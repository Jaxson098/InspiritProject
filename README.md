### Abstract ###
This paper applies machine learning to the study of American presidential elections, combining predictive
modeling with analysis of voter reasoning and the structural constraints of the two-party system. Using
polling data, candidate demographic information, and more than 1.8 million words of candidate speech
transcripts, a random forest model was trained on eight elections (1980–2020). The model achieved a
mean error of 1.26% in predicting vote share, outperforming prominent methodologies such as ABC
news’s FiveThirtyEight. More importantly, analysis of feature importances revealed that voter
decision-making is often driven less by issue alignment than candidate demographics, with experience,
education, and incumbency playing decisive roles, particularly for Democratic candidates. The findings
also highlight a broader democratic dilemma: as public opinion has diversified and polarized, consensus
on major issues has eroded, leaving a growing fraction of voters unrepresented by either major party. This
disconnect forces many citizens to vote along abstract partisan lines rather than substantive policy
alignment, creating what we term an “artificial democracy.” By quantifying these patterns, this study not
only advances election prediction but also exposes the limitations of the current “left wing” or “right
wing” electoral framework in representing the complexity of the American electorate.
